Updating your regulatory documents
European regulations require specific documents to ensure good practices and safety in wineries. They must be updated every year and attest to the quality of your work and your working environment:
– An HACCP plan (Hazard Analysis Critical Control Point): mandatory for all wineries producing wine intended for sale. It guarantees the sanitary safety of the products placed on the market by preventing any risk that could harm consumer health.
– A DUER (Single Document for the Evaluation of Occupational Risks): mandatory from the moment you have at least one employee at any point during the year. It ensures their safety in the workplace.

These documents follow strict standards and must be drafted with categories imposed by regulations, with the highest level of precision, to guarantee the safety and protection of your products, your employees, and yourself.

In most of the wineries I have worked with, I often found either the complete absence of regulatory documents, or at best, documents that had not been updated for several years. Documents that are outdated or imprecise lead to:
– A potential lack of control over the quality of your products.
– Endangering your employees.
– A very high legal and financial risk for you in case of an incident.

Setting up trials and sensory analyses
Before investing in a new winemaking approach, a change in your winemaking process or parameters, or a new grape variety, it can be valuable to run a small-scale trial. When properly designed, it is a real pillar of innovation and improvement.
– A poorly structured trial does not guarantee that your results will be correct or extrapolable.
– A trial must be associated with a thoughtful selection of parameters to collect and a coherent collection method, in order to reach your objective.
– The data from your trial must be analysed using appropriate statistical methods to ensure reliable interpretation.
– A trial inevitably comes with limitations related to method and extrapolation, which must be understood to correctly apply the results at full scale.

I have noticed that many small and medium-sized wineries believe trials are out of their reach, while others try to conduct them but struggle with the level of rigour required:
– It is wrong to believe that a trial must be expensive; it can be adapted to your means and will help you avoid future mistakes and losses.
– It is wrong to believe that a trial is difficult to carry out; it can be designed, managed, analysed, and interpreted remotely without the need for a qualified person on site.

Quality audit, training, restructuring
In a winery, every detail matters. A single oversight, mistake, or negligence can lead to the loss of an entire year of work and investment. Sometimes, you may observe consequences in the vineyard or the wine without being able to identify the exact cause, increasing the risk of repeating the same mistakes the following year.
– Decisions must be coherent with the entire process, taking into account what has been done in the past as well as what will be done in the future.
– Equipment and machines must be used correctly.
– And above all, analyses must be mastered.

Throughout my experiences and visits, I have had the opportunity to identify numerous points where quality control was lacking: ineffective cleaning protocols, inconsistent use of oenological products, incorrect sampling methods, inaccurate analyses, unjustified fertilisation, improper pruning… Faced with this, it is essential to:
– Train and inform teams about the evolution of scientific knowledge and new discoveries, which sometimes contradict older beliefs.
– Restructure processes, environments, methods, and protocols to achieve coherence and prevent risks.
